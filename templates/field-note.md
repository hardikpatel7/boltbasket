# Template: Field Note

The shorter format. ~600–900 words. One per week, paired with one long-form post.

A field note has **one sharp idea, fast.** It's the punchy companion to the long-form deep dive. SEO-friendly when titled well. Lower production cost. Higher publish frequency without burnout.

---

## When to write a field note vs. long-form

- **Long-form:** Story-driven, multi-section, full BoltBasket scene-setting, requires diagrams.
- **Field note:** Single insight, single point, often opinionated. May reference BoltBasket but doesn't need a full story arc.

If a long-form draft is coming in at <1,200 words and feels complete, ship it as a field note. Don't pad.

---

## Field note structures (pick one)

### Pattern A: The decision framework

A short, opinionated guide to making one specific decision well.

- **Title pattern:** "X vs Y: How to actually decide" / "When to use X (and when not to)"
- **Structure:** problem → criteria → decision → BoltBasket example as illustration
- **Example:** "ETL vs ELT: A 5-Minute Decision Framework"

### Pattern B: The corrected misconception

You've seen the wrong take a hundred times. Correct it concisely.

- **Title pattern:** "What I got wrong about X" / "X is not what you think it is"
- **Structure:** the common belief → why it's incomplete → the better mental model → BoltBasket example
- **Example:** "What I Got Wrong About Data Fabric in My First Year"

### Pattern C: The single concept demystified

One concept, no fluff, written for the early-career reader.

- **Title pattern:** "X, plainly" / "Understanding X without the buzzwords"
- **Structure:** what it is in one sentence → the simplest example → when it matters → when it doesn't
- **Example:** "Materialized Views, Plainly"

### Pattern D: The opinion piece

A short take with a clear position.

- **Title pattern:** "Why I think X" / "Stop doing X"
- **Structure:** position → evidence → counter-arguments → what to do instead
- **Example:** "Stop Calling Your dbt Project a 'Semantic Layer'"

## Pre-draft checklist

- **The single point:** What is the one thing the reader should walk away believing?
- **The hook:** What's the first sentence?
- **The BoltBasket touch:** Is there a brief reference back to the universe? (Optional but preferred — keeps the universe alive.)
- **The honesty beat:** Even at 700 words, where do I admit nuance?

---

## Length & rhythm

- **600–900 words.** Hard ceiling at 1,000.
- **Short paragraphs.** 2–3 sentences each. Mobile reading.
- **One small visual or none.** A field note doesn't need a diagram. If it has one, it should be the simplest possible.
- **No required 6-section structure.** Field notes can be 3 sections, or just flow as connected prose.

---

## Title patterns

Field notes lean into searchability and curiosity in equal measure.

**Good:**
- "ETL vs ELT: When Each Actually Wins"
- "Materialized Views, Plainly"
- "Stop Calling Your dbt Project a Semantic Layer"
- "The Three Definitions of 'Active User' (And Why You Have All Three)"

**Avoid:**
- "A Quick Note on Data Lineage" (no signal)
- "Some Thoughts on ETL" (no point of view)
- "Field Note: Materialized Views" (don't label it "field note" in the title)

---

## End-of-field-note elements

Lighter than long-form. Just:

1. **One link** to the corresponding deep-dive (current or upcoming) — "I went deeper into this in [previous post]" or "More on this next week."
2. **Connect line** — same as long-form.

No GitHub repo link required for a field note unless it's a code-heavy piece.

---

## Common failure modes for field notes

- **Stretching to long-form length.** If you have more to say, write the long-form post separately.
- **Burying the point.** A field note's job is to make one point well. Lead with the conclusion if needed.
- **No opinion.** A field note without a position reads like a Wikipedia stub. Have a take.
- **Over-referencing the BoltBasket universe.** A field note can stand alone. Don't force the lore in.
